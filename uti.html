UTI

A urinary tract infection (UTI) is an infection that mainly affects the lower part of the urinary system: bladder, urethra and sometimes even the kidneys. Typical symptoms are a lasting need to urinate, together with a burning pain when doing so. However, frequently the affected person can urinate only in small amounts, which eventually becomes very frustrating. Women, who have a higher chance to contract UTIs due to their physiology (closeness of urethra and anus), additionally suffer from pelvic pain. A UTI is a tedious and distressing disease.

It is estimated that 90% of UTIs are caused by E. coli bacteria. These bacteria reside in the bowel tract, amongst others, and are found in feces. Therefore, bad hygiene, sexual activity and the use of certain birth control types are considered risk factors and favor the contraction of a UTI.

Because a UTI can spread to the kidneys, and in some cases become life threatening, it is very commonly treated with antibiotics. However, roughly 25% of women affected by a UTI get the disease again within the following year. This high chance of recurrence and the possibility to develop a chronic UTI makes the administration of antibiotics less effective and is a dangerous strategy, since the patient will develop an antibiotic resistance. Hence alternative treatments for UTI are necessary.
